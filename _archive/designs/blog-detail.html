The difference between UX & UI: a simple guide.

Two acronyms, one product, endless confusion. Here's how we untangle the difference in our studio — and why getting it right shapes every decision that follows.

Maal Khan Design Lead
Published22 Apr 2026
Read time6 minutes
In this article
  1. TL;DR
  2. What is UX?
  3. What is UI?
  4. How we work with both
  5. The takeaway
Share

— 01TL;DR

UX is how a product works. UI is how a product looks and how you touch it. They're inseparable in the final experience, but they're shaped by different questions — and by different people, working at different moments.

— 02What is UX?

UX — user experience — is the architecture of an interaction. It's the chain of decisions a person makes to get from "I have a problem" to "I solved it." Good UX makes that chain feel inevitable. Bad UX makes it feel like a tax.

When we run discovery on a new product, the UX questions we ask sound nothing like design questions. They sound like research questions. Who is this for? What were they doing five minutes before they opened this? What will they be doing five minutes after they close it? UX lives in the answers.

UX is not a screen. It's the through-line that makes a screen worth visiting in the first place.

— Maal, in studio notes, 2024

— 03What is UI?

UI — user interface — is the surface where the experience meets the human. It's the colour of the button, the rhythm of a list, the feel of a swipe. It's craft. It's the part most people see, even when they don't have language for it.

The mistake is treating UI as decoration. A button that looks right but takes 800ms to respond doesn't have a UI problem — it has a UX one. A button that responds in 80ms but is the wrong shade of grey doesn't have a UX problem — it has a UI one. Both can sink a product.

Three things UI gets blamed for that are actually UX

  • "This page feels cluttered." → Too many decisions, not too many pixels.
  • "I can't find anything." → Information architecture, not visual hierarchy.
  • "It's slow." → Perceived performance, not animation polish.

— 04How we work with both

In our studio, UX and UI run on parallel rails. Discovery and IA work happens on whiteboards and in interview transcripts. Visual systems happen in Figma and the browser. They meet in critique — every Wednesday, religiously — where we line up flows next to screens and ask whether they're telling the same story.

The most expensive mistake we see at agencies is letting one discipline outrun the other. UX without UI ships beautifully-architected products that nobody wants to use. UI without UX ships beautifully-detailed products that nobody can use. The work is to keep them in lockstep, even when the deadline says "just open Figma."

→ Studio practice

We never present UI without the flow it lives inside. Every Figma file in our studio opens on the journey, not the screen. The screen is the artifact; the journey is the design.

— 05The takeaway

Don't hire a UX designer to make your product pretty. Don't hire a UI designer to fix your information architecture. And if you're hiring one person to do both — which is most of us, most of the time — make sure they know which question they're answering at any given moment.

Because the question you're answering tells you which tools to pick up. And the tool you pick up shapes the answer you find.
